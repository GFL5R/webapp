Scenes

Scenes are the building blocks of play. A scene sets expectations for pacing, time, turn structure, what risks are in focus, and how outcomes are decided. The GM declares when a scene starts and ends.

Key Properties for Any Scene

  • Purpose: what the scene is about.
  • Duration: how time passes in fiction.
  • Turn order: whether actions are freeform or structured.
  • Initiative: how action priority is set when structured.
  • Common actions: typical moves and checks that resolve tension.
  • Outcomes: what ends the scene or changes its mode.

Structured Time

Conflicts like Intrigues, Duels, Skirmishes, and Mass Battles use structured time: characters act in a regulated order, and each is limited in what they can accomplish before others have a chance to respond.

Actions are discrete tasks you undertake during a scene, usually by making a check. Turns are your chance to perform an action and accomplish small goals. Rounds are the span of time in which each character takes one turn.

Initiative

Initiative determines the order in which characters act during structured time. When a conflict begins, each participant makes an initiative check using a specific Skill:

Scene TypeInitiative Skill
IntriguesInsight
DuelsResolve
Skirmishes & BreachesTactics
Mass BattlesCommand
Electronic WarfareComputers

Your base initiative value depends on readiness: Focus if prepared, Vigilance if surprised. On success, add 1 plus bonus successes to your base. Characters act in descending order.

Approach Effects

The Approach you use for your initiative check grants a benefit throughout the conflict:

Resilience

Others cannot spend Opportunity on Attack/Scheme checks targeting you to inflict critical strikes or conditions.

Swiftness

Perform one additional action that does not require a check (cannot share a type with another action this turn).

Power

When you succeed, count each Strife symbol on your kept dice as one additional bonus success.

Precision

Increase TN of Attack/Scheme checks targeting you by 1 (or 2 if Discipline rank total is 4+).

Fortune

You do not receive Strife from Strife symbols on your kept dice. Other Strife sources still apply.

Narrative Scenes

Narrative scenes are for roleplay, exploration, and everyday interactions without formal timing. Time passes at whatever pace feels natural. There is no turn order: conversation and actions flow organically.

Make checks when uncertainty matters. Use these scenes to create advantages through preparation that can be carried into later scenes. The scene ends when goals are met, when stakes escalate into Intrigue or Skirmish, or when the fiction demands a time skip.

◇ EXAMPLE: A QUIET BRIEFING

The team meets a handler. You ask questions, make a Precision plus Insight check to read between the lines, and set an approach for the next operation. No turn order is used.

Downtime Scenes

Downtime scenes represent extended periods of rest, travel, maintenance, learning, and light activity. These scenes can span days, weeks, or even months. Common uses include recovering Endurance or Composure, training Skills or advancing Disciplines, and managing long-term projects like crafting, repairs, research, or networking.

◇ EXAMPLE: OVERLAND TRAVEL

You spend two weeks crossing a collapsed zone. One Fieldcraft check sets the overall success of foraging and route choice. The GM summarizes events. You reach the objective with minor Strife cleared.

Intrigues

Intrigues handle structured social conflict where reactions must be ordered and stakes are contested without violence. Initiative is determined by Insight checks.

On your turn you can Press to advance your position, Deflect to resist pressure, Observe to gather tells and openings, Assist others, or Withdraw to concede some stakes.

◇ EXAMPLE: HOSTILE NEGOTIATION

You argue terms with a mercenary captain. Initiative is set by Insight checks: you use Precision to gain the defensive bonus, while the captain uses Power. On your turn you Press with TN 3 to secure better payment. The captain Deflects, raising your future TN unless you Observe and create an opening.

Duels

Duels are one-on-one fights with special rules for tempo, space, and openings. Initiative is determined by Resolve checks. During a duel you select Approaches, set stances, create openings and exploit them, and manage Strife carefully since mistakes compound quickly in single combat.

Skirmishes

Skirmishes are the default form of fighting between multiple combatants. Initiative is determined by Tactics checks. On your turn you can move, take actions, and react using combat actions. Track Endurance, Composure, and Strife throughout the fight.

◇ EXAMPLE: STREET AMBUSH

Initiative is set by Tactics checks. You use Swiftness to gain an extra non-check action per turn. You move to cover and fire an Assault Rifle attack at TN 3. Your partner Assists to lower your TN. The GM applies Threat and Signature from the weapon entry.

Mass Battles

Mass battles handle fights too large for skirmish scale, where squads, companies, or entire armies clash. Initiative is determined by Command checks. Units resolve actions through orders and morale checks. Key engagements can zoom in to skirmish scale as spotlight scenes inside the larger battle.

Special Scenes

Breaches

A breach is a special skirmish where defenders hold a threshold without line of sight and attackers must enter, acquire targets, and engage. Breaches unfold in three phases:

PHASE 1

Preparation

Semi-structured. Every member of both sides takes one action to prepare. Attackers and defenders act without knowing what the other side has done.

PHASE 2

Door Kick

The barrier is breached and both sides make contact. Initiative is determined by Tactics checks. Attackers suffer a TN 2 penalty to any attack during this phase, representing disorientation crossing a fatal funnel.

PHASE 3

Open Combat

After the first round, the breach transitions to open combat. Initiative is preserved and combat proceeds as a normal Skirmish. All effects and conditions carry forward from Phase 2.

Electronic Warfare Scenes

Electronic warfare scenes focus on intrusion, defense, and manipulation of electronic systems. A solo hacker in a lightly defended network can be handled as a Narrative scene. When structure is needed, the GM frames it as a Conflict scene. When two hackers clash, it becomes a Hacker Duel.

◇ EXAMPLE: SIGNAL DUEL

You begin by quietly Cracking a control net during a Narrative scene. When your Heat crosses 4, a defender jacks in and the GM shifts into a Hacker Duel. At the start of each round, Heat climbs. You Disrupt the defender's drone relay, inflicting Dazed. The defender Pauses to bleed Heat and Strife, buying time while security converges on your traced location.

Starting and Ending Scenes

The GM frames the scene with purpose and scope. Signal when stakes change. End a scene when its goals are resolved or when a different scene type is more appropriate.

  • Start: declare type, set any special rules, clarify time scale, and establish what success looks like.
  • End: goals met, concessions made, or escalation to a new scene type.
